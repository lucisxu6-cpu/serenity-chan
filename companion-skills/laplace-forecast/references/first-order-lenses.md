# First-Order Lenses

Use these lenses together when the question involves real people, institutions, markets, or social systems. Do not force every lens every time; choose the fewest lenses that still explain the system honestly.

## Economics and Incentives

Ask:

- Who pays, who earns, who subsidizes, who loses?
- Where does margin pool or vanish?
- What gets cheaper, what stays scarce?
- What are the switching costs, coordination costs, and opportunity costs?

This lens is first-order when the question involves jobs, industries, pricing, business models, or adoption speed.

## Social Structure and Roles

Ask:

- Which groups, classes, professions, households, or organizations shape the outcome?
- What roles do trust, hierarchy, family, networks, or gatekeepers play?
- Whose status rises or falls if the change happens?

This lens matters when formal incentives alone do not explain behavior.

## Social Psychology and Emotion

Ask:

- What are people really trying to avoid or protect: shame, fear, exclusion, uncertainty, loss of control?
- What are they chasing: convenience, status, belonging, hope, identity, relief?
- Is the system driven by imitation, panic, resentment, aspiration, or fatigue?

This lens is often decisive in consumer behavior, politics, labor, social media, and personal decision-making.

## Behavioral Mechanics

Ask:

- How do habit, friction, defaults, present bias, and loss aversion shape behavior?
- Does the change demand discipline or merely remove friction?
- Are people choosing actively, or falling into the easiest path?

This lens helps explain why technically better systems still fail in practice.

## Institutions, Power, and Rules

Ask:

- Which laws, policies, gatekeepers, or platform rules can veto the outcome?
- Who has enforcement power?
- Is there strategic ambiguity, policy lag, procurement friction, censorship, or compliance drag?

This lens is first-order for finance, health, education, labor markets, AI, and geopolitics.

## Technology and Cost Curves

Ask:

- What technical capability is actually improving?
- Is the improvement on quality, speed, price, reliability, or integration?
- Is the limiting factor model quality, data, compute, workflow fit, or trust?

Use this lens to separate demos from deployable systems.

## Operations and Reality

Ask:

- What must happen in the real world for the forecast to come true?
- Who has to execute, maintain, deliver, train, support, or absorb mistakes?
- Where are the failure points in logistics, onboarding, quality control, or responsibility?

This lens is crucial whenever the forecast crosses from information into real-world delivery.

## Capital and Distribution

Ask:

- Who controls customer access?
- Who has the cash runway to survive the transition?
- Which channels, platforms, communities, or partnerships determine distribution?

Many good ideas lose because they have no route to customers or no staying power.

## Geopolitics and Security

Ask:

- What are the deterrence structures, alliance commitments, red lines, and escalation ladders?
- Where could miscalculation overwhelm rational restraint?
- Which actors need ambiguity, and which need signaling?

Use this lens for war, sanctions, trade conflict, supply chains, and critical infrastructure.

## Choosing Lenses

Default pattern by topic:

- Personal strategy: economics + social psychology + behavior + operations
- Careers and jobs: economics + technology + institutions + social psychology
- Industry forecasts: economics + technology + distribution + operations
- Political or geopolitical risk: institutions + social psychology + geopolitics + incentives
- AI adoption: technology + economics + trust + workflow operations + regulation

## Lens Arbitration

When lenses conflict, do not average them. Resolve them by role:

- `Direction`: which lens best explains the long-run pull of the system?
- `Timing`: which lens determines how fast change can happen?
- `Veto`: which lens contains the actor or rule that can block the outcome entirely?
- `Magnitude`: which lens determines how large the effect can become if it happens?

Practical rules:

- Institutions usually beat desire when formal permission is required.
- Operations usually beat strategy when physical execution is the bottleneck.
- Social psychology usually beats abstract efficiency in visible, low-friction consumer adoption.
- Economics usually beats fashion over the long run if there is no durable veto.
- Distribution and capital often decide winners among technically similar options.

If two lenses conflict, say which one dominates and why.

When user utility profiles differ materially, do not force one answer. Branch the recommendation by profile:

- `risk-averse`
- `balanced`
- `aggressive` or `high-upside`

Only collapse to one recommendation when the action is robust across profiles.

## Reflexivity

Some systems change because people believe they are changing.

Ask:

- Who will see or sense this forecast?
- How will major actors adapt once the trend becomes legible?
- Does attention create crowding, panic, imitation, defensive regulation, or speculative overbuild?
- Will the leading actor hide, accelerate, bluff, or slow-roll in response?

Reflexivity is first-order in:

- markets and asset pricing
- platform ecosystems
- politics and public opinion
- geopolitics and deterrence
- career and educational stampedes

If the forecast itself changes the system, include that in the base case rather than treating it as a side note.

Inject reflexivity twice:

- early, when building the causal map
- late, as a final sweep before locking the answer

If reflexivity only appears at the end, the model is probably underbuilt.
